## 1. Current Landscape

### The Numbers (2025-2026)

- ~36 waiting families per 1 infant placed for adoption
- ~18,000-20,000 domestic infant adoptions per year in the US
- Average wait: 1-3 years (agency only) or 6-18 months (agency + active
  self-marketing + consultant)
- 90-95% of private domestic infant adoptions are now open to some degree
- Cost range: $30,000-$50,000 typical for private domestic

### What's Working Right Now

- **Multi-channel approach is essential.** Families who combine agency
  placement, adoption consultant networking, a personal website, and social
  media presence match significantly faster than agency-only families.
- **Video content is no longer optional.** Short-form video (Reels, TikTok,
  YouTube Shorts) is the biggest tactical shift. Expectant parents want to _see_
  your family in motion, not just photos.
- **Adoption consultants have gone mainstream.** Consultants network your
  profile across 20-50+ agencies/attorneys simultaneously. Most successful
  families now use at least one.
- **Authenticity beats polish.** Expectant parents consistently report being
  drawn to genuine, imperfect, real-life content over staged, Pinterest-perfect
  presentations.
- **Openness is the expectation.** Expectant parents increasingly _require_
  meaningful openness as a condition. Families who demonstrate genuine comfort
  with open adoption match faster.
- **Word of mouth still matters enormously.** Personal networks, church
  communities, and friends-of-friends remain a significant source of
  connections.

### Post-Dobbs Reality

- No dramatic surge in placements despite predictions
- Medication abortion access via telehealth has offset many state bans
- More waiting families entered the pool post-Dobbs, actually _increasing_
  competition
- The political framing of "adopt instead" has made some in the adoption
  community uncomfortable — be aware of this sensitivity

### Utah-Specific Notes

- Utah has relatively adoption-friendly laws
- **Utah Adoption Specialists (UAS)** provides mental health services and consulting for your home study — they are NOT a placement agency and do not facilitate matches
- You will need a **separate placement agency, adoption attorney, or adoption consultant** to actually match with an expectant parent
- Utah's LDS community provides a strong natural network for word-of-mouth
- Utah allows birth mother living expenses within reason
- Consent can be signed relatively quickly in Utah (verify current timing with your placement provider)
- If matching out of state, ICPC (Interstate Compact) will add 1-3 weeks of waiting in the birth state

**Your v1 strategy: private self-matching.** This means your website, social media, and personal networks ARE your placement path. This raises the stakes on:

- **Social media consistency** — 3-4x/week minimum, sustained over months

### Messaging Framework

**Lead with:**

- Your family's warmth and daily life
- Your children's excitement about a sibling
- Your home, neighborhood, and community
- Genuine openness to birth family relationship
- What life looks like day-to-day (not highlight reel)

**Avoid:**

- Savior/rescue language ("save a child," "give a better life")
- Desperation or entitlement ("we deserve this," "it's our turn")
- Empty nursery photos or countdown content
- Centering your pain/infertility story (if applicable)
- Overly religious language that could feel exclusionary
- Anything that positions the expectant parent as a means to your end

**Language Guide:**

- Say "expectant mother/parent" (not "birth mother" — that's only after
  placement)
- Say "making an adoption plan" (not "giving up")
- Say "place for adoption" (not "give away")
- Center the child's wellbeing in all messaging
- Acknowledge that adoption involves loss alongside joy

### LDS-Specific Considerations

- Your faith community is a massive asset — LDS networks are tight and word
  travels fast
- Present your faith authentically but not exclusively — expectant parents of
  all backgrounds (or no faith) should feel welcome
- Avoid framing adoption as "God's plan" for the expectant mother — this can
  feel dismissive of her grief and agency
- Your ward, stake, and extended LDS network can amplify your reach
  significantly
- Many LDS families have adopted — lean on that community for advice and
  referrals
- Consider that some expectant parents may specifically seek or avoid LDS
  families — be authentic and let the right match find you

  enough to have input)
- **Set the account to be findable but not fully public** if possible — or
  accept public but limit identifying details
- **Watermark photos** to discourage misuse

## 5. Family Letter

### Structure for Your "Dear Expectant Parent" Letter

1. **Opening (2-3 sentences):** Acknowledge their courage. Express gratitude
   that they're reading your letter. Validate that this is their decision and
   you respect it.

2. **Who you are (3-4 sentences):** Brief intro to your family — who you are as
   a couple, your two kids, the vibe of your home.

3. **Why adoption (2-3 sentences):** Honest, brief explanation. Focus on what
   you want to _give_, not what you lack.

4. **What life would look like (1 paragraph):** Paint a specific, sensory
   picture. Saturday mornings, holiday traditions, bedtime routines. Make them
   _see_ their child in your home.

5. **Your children as siblings (2-3 sentences):** How your daughter and son feel
   about adoption. Their excitement. What kind of siblings they'd be.

6. **Open adoption commitment (2-3 sentences):** What level of openness you're
   comfortable with. Be specific and honest — don't over-promise.

7. **Closing (2-3 sentences):** Reiterate respect for their decision. Express
   hope without pressure. Make it clear you honor their agency.

**Length:** 500-800 words (1-2 pages). Shorter is better — attention spans are
short.

**Tone:** Like talking to a friend. Warm, honest, not formal. Read it out loud —
if it sounds stiff, rewrite it.

- Natural lighting preferred
- Add captions/subtitles (many watch on mute)
- Somewhere between iPhone-casual and professionally produced
- Authenticity > production value

## 7. Fundraising Options

### Should You Fundraise?

This is a personal decision. Consider:

- Private domestic adoption typically costs $30,000-$50,000
- The federal adoption tax credit (~$17,000) reimburses a large portion but
  comes AFTER finalization
- Fundraising is common and not something to feel awkward about
- Your LDS community and ward are likely natural supporters

### If Yes — Recommended Approach

**Platform:** AdoptTogether (adopttogether.org)

- Adoption-specific, donations are tax-deductible
- Credible and well-known in the adoption space
- Supplement with GoFundMe or Facebook fundraisers for broader reach

**Top fundraising methods (by ROI):**

1. **Personal letter campaign** — Write 100-200 physical letters to friends,
   family, ward members, colleagues. Include your story and a return envelope.
   Response rates of 30-50% are common. Average gift $50-200. A 150-letter
   campaign can raise $7,500-$22,000. _This is consistently the single
   highest-ROI fundraising activity._

2. **Ward/stake fundraising event** — Dinner, auction, or service project. Your
   LDS community will likely rally hard. $5,000-$15,000 potential.

3. **Both Hands Foundation** (bothhands.org) — A team of volunteers does a
   service project (home repair for a widow/elderly person), sponsors donate
   per-project. Tax-deductible. Typical raise: $5,000-$15,000. Aligns
   beautifully with LDS service values.

4. **T-shirt / merchandise campaign** — Design adoption-themed shirts via
   Bonfire. Sell for $25-35 with $10-15 profit. $2,000-$5,000.

5. **Online crowdfunding** — AdoptTogether + social media sharing. Front-load
   with 10-20 committed donors in the first 48 hours.

## 11. Privacy & Child Protection Strategy

Since protecting your children is a priority, here's a dedicated strategy:

### Tiered Privacy Approach

**Tier 1: Public Adoption Accounts (Instagram, Website, Profile Sites)**

- Use first names only (no last name anywhere)
- No school names, team names, or identifiable activity locations
- No photos showing your street address or house number
- Disable geolocation on all posts
- Consider showing children in activities (from behind, side angles, in motion)
  rather than clear portraits — OR use clear photos but control all other
  identifying info
- Never post your children's birth dates (use ages only)
- Watermark photos with a subtle overlay

**Tier 2: Adoption Profile Book / Agency Profile**

- First names are fine
- More personal detail is appropriate here (it's a controlled audience)
- Still omit school names and specific addresses
- Your agency can advise on what's standard

**Tier 3: Personal Social Media**

- Lock down all personal accounts (private/friends-only)
- Audit existing posts for identifying information
- Ask family members to not tag your children or share your adoption content
  with identifying details
- Review tagged photos and remove tags as needed

### Digital Safety Checklist

- [ ] Set all personal social accounts to private
- [ ] Audit old posts for school names, address visibility, routine patterns
- [ ] Brief extended family on what NOT to share publicly
- [ ] Set up a Google Voice number for adoption contacts (not your personal
      cell)
- [ ] Create a dedicated email for adoption (not your personal email)
- [ ] Disable location services on your phone's camera
- [ ] Talk to your 10-year-old about what she's comfortable with
- [ ] Decide on your family's photo comfort level and be consistent

### Scam Awareness

- NEVER send money directly to someone claiming to be an expectant parent
- ALL financial arrangements go through your agency or attorney
- Be cautious of DMs from unknown accounts
- If something feels off, trust your gut and consult your agency
- Social media marketing unfortunately makes families targets for scams — be
  prepared emotionally
